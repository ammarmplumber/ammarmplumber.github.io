Philosophy of Finance — Spring 2023

Teaching

This is the second seminar I taught for the Collegium Institute at the University of Pennsylvania. It was the most attended among the Professions and the Good Life seminars.

In this course, we consider the role finance plays in supporting (or undermining) the common good. We also take a look at the 2008 financial crisis and assess the extent to which epistemic vices (incompetence, financial illiteracy) were responsible, as opposed to greed or appetite for risk. Other topics include medieval debates on usury, perspectives on debt accumulation, and financial exclusion.

Syllabus

Week 1 — Logic and Function of Finance

Reading:

  • “The Logic and Function of Finance” || Pierre de Lauzun, Finance: A Christian Perspective (2021)

Week 2 — Trustworthiness and Epistemic Virtue

Reading:

  • “Introduction” || Boudewijn de Bruin, Ethics and the Global Financial Crisis: Why Incompetence is Worse than Greed (2014)

Week 3 — Clients and Banks

Reading:

  • “Case Study I: primes and subprimes” || Boudewijn de Bruin, Ethics and the Global Financial Crisis: Why Incompetence is Worse than Greed (2014)
  • “Case Study II: nerds and quants” || Boudewijn de Bruin, Ethics and the Global Financial Crisis: Why Incompetence is Worse than Greed (2014)

Week 4 — Socially Responsible Investing

Reading:

  • “Sustainable Finance and Responsible Investors” || Boudewijn de Bruin, The Business of Liberty: Freedom and Information in Ethics, Politics, and Law (2022)

Week 5 — Medieval Debates on Usury, part 1

Guest lecturer: Prof. Ivan Luzardo-Luna, Penn Economics

Reading:

  • “The Medieval Origins of the Financial Revolution: Usury, Rentes, and Negotiability” || John H. Munro, The International History Review, Vol. 25, No. 3 (2003), pp. 505-562

Week 6 — Medieval Debates on Usury, part 2

Reading:

  • “Usury and Just Compensation: Religious and Financial Ethics in Historical Perspective” || Constant Mews and Ibraham Abraham, Journal of Business Ethics Vol. 72 (2007), pp. 1–15
